The Quantum Theory of Politics

It's a long-established fact that any "good" politician can change his ideological beliefs as quickly as popular opinion can shift in any direction. For a while, I was alright with this: I could understand the how, if not the why. I could predict the behaviour of politicians, predict their "long-held beliefs" by trending hashtags.

But lately, my model has broken down. I've had to revise my position. I've had to re-evaluate how politicians work. And I've come to a startling conclusion:

Politics are governed by the same principles as is the universe under quantum field theory.

The basic concepts behind this theory are as follows:

Complementarity
Similarly to light being both a particle and a wave, the average politician is both a conservative and a liberal, depending on the situation. It is not that they are one or the other, it is not that they are one then the other; they are both at the same time.
Entanglement
The act of observing can not be separated from the outcome of that observation. By asking a politician how they feel about an issue, you inevitably affect how they feel about it. More concretely, a politician will feel every possible way about an issue until the moment they are asked about it, at which point the many opinions collapse into the single answer most likely to please the asker. Caveat: there are those who believe that there exist infinitely many political realms, and that any given politician holds a slightly different opinion in each one. This, of course, is absurd, and is only included in this list so as to give equal weight to the opinions of crackpots.
Non-causality
In traditional politics, the cause of getting the most votes leads to the effect of being considered the most electable condidate. Lately, though, we've seen that being considered the most electable candidate actually produces the effect of receiving the most votes.
Probability
A politician's ideals can be expressed only in terms of probability, not certainty. While some opinions are inherently less likely that others, no opinion can be thought of to have zero probability.
Uncertainty
The rules of quantum politics dictate that no entity can ever know both what a politician's current position on an issue is and where that position will be at a future date. This is affectionally called the "principle uncertainty principle".
